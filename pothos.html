Epipremnum Aureum

Pothos, Devil's Ivy

About
A care guide for pothos plants is almost redunant, as they are incredibly easy to keep alive. (In fact, they are often referred to as "devil's ivy because they are so hard to kill! They need only sparse waterings and these plants will thrive in almost any setting.
Pot
Pothos plants don't actually have to be planted in soil; they do very well just placed in a container with water. If you do decide to plant them in soil, any potting mix is fine.
Pothos do well in a wide variety of light conditions and are therefore an excellent option for even low-light homes - in fact, it is best to avoid direct sunlight. A variegated pothos placed in low-light conditions may lose some of its white color because only the green part of the leaf can absorb light. On the other hand, if you notice that your pothos' leaves look paler than usual, that's a sign that your plant is getting too much light.
Light
Propagating pothos is extremely easy. You can simply remove a leaf and node and place in soil or water to root. Be mindful however that cuttings started in one growing medium have a hard time switching to the other.

Toxicity
While pothos plants are an easy to care for houseplant, you do need to be aware that they are poisonous. Though rarely fatal, the plant can cause irritation and vomiting if ingested due to the fact that it contains calcium oxalates. It is considered toxic to cats, dogs and children.
Vine
Naturally, pothos plants are trailers. As trailers, they are great as indoor hanging plants or placed up high on shelves. Pinching back stems at the tips ensures that your plant doesn't become too thin and stringy.
